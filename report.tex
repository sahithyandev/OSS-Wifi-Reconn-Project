\section{Introduction}

Wi-Fi reconnaissance means gathering information about nearby wireless networks. It is the first step in a security assessment: find access points, check their configuration, and identify weaknesses.

For this assignment I used the Aircrack-ng suite, a set of command-line tools for sniffing wireless traffic and capturing packets.

\subsection{Tools Used}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{airmon-ng}: puts the wireless adapter into monitor mode.
    \item \textbf{airodump-ng}: scans nearby networks and captures packets.
    \item \textbf{aireplay-ng}: sends deauth frames to trigger a WPA/WPA2 handshake.
\end{itemize}

The goal: collect SSIDs, BSSIDs, channels, encryption types, and signal strengths from networks in range.

\section{Preparation}

Some setup was required before starting.

The MacBook's built-in Wi-Fi card does not support monitor mode or packet injection, so I used a Lenovo Vostro 15 running Ubuntu 24.04. Its Realtek RTL8852BE card supports both.

\subsection{Background}

\subsubsection{802.11 Wireless Communication}

Wi-Fi uses IEEE 802.11 over shared radio frequencies. In \emph{monitor mode}, a device receives all frames without joining a network. 802.11 has three frame types: management (beacons, probe requests, authentication, deauthentication), control, and data. Under WPA and WPA2, management frames are plaintext and unauthenticated. This is why the deauth attack works.

\subsubsection{WPA, WPA2, and WPA3}

\textbf{WPA} (2003) replaced WEP using TKIP, which wraps RC4 with per-packet key mixing. TKIP is weak and should not be used.

\textbf{WPA2} (IEEE 802.11i, 2004) replaced TKIP with AES-CCMP. It has two modes: Personal (PSK) and Enterprise (802.1X). With the four-way handshake, PSK mode can be attacked offline.

\textbf{WPA3} (2018) introduced SAE (Simultaneous Authentication of Equals), replacing the PSK handshake. SAE provides forward secrecy and prevents offline dictionary attacks. WPA3 also requires Protected Management Frames (PMF / 802.11w), which blocks the deauth attack (Section~\ref{sec:deauth}).

\subsubsection{The WPA/WPA2 Four-Way Handshake}

When a client joins a WPA2 network, the AP and client exchange four messages to derive a session key (PTK) from the passphrase and two random nonces. The passphrase is never sent directly. The handshake includes a MIC computed from the passphrase. An attacker captures the handshake, guesses a passphrase, recomputes the MIC, and checks for a match. This can be done offline, without further contact with the network.

\section{Activities and Results}

\subsection{Passive Network Scanning}

In passive scanning, the adapter listens without transmitting or joining any network. This reveals BSSIDs, channels, encryption types, and connected clients.

I scanned with \texttt{airodump-ng}:

\begin{lstlisting}
sudo airodump-ng wlp2s0mon
\end{lstlisting}

This outputs each nearby AP with its SSID, BSSID, channel, encryption, signal, and clients. Six sessions on 2026-05-16 (12:27--12:54, ~27 minutes) found \textbf{39 unique access points} and ~\textbf{192,000 frames} (31,000 beacons, 161,000 data).

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{32 networks} used WPA2, \textbf{1} (Redmi A5) used WPA3. Most routers and ISPs do not support WPA3.
    \item \textbf{4 networks} were open, including the ``Redmi A1+'' hotspot.

    \item Some APs ran mixed WPA/WPA2 mode.
    \item Several mobile hotspots were visible: ``Thavanathan's iPhone'', ``Galaxy M0298c8'', ``TimBaTTuK's S25 Ultra''.

Open networks expose all traffic in plaintext. WPA2 is acceptable with a strong passphrase, but mixed WPA/WPA2 falls back to TKIP, which is weak. Hidden SSIDs offer no real protection; the AP still beacons and \texttt{airodump-ng} captured them. IoT devices are easy to identify by SSID and typically run outdated firmware.

\subsection{Deauthentication Attack}
\label{sec:deauth}

I ran a deauth attack against my own hotspot to force clients to reconnect and trigger the WPA handshake. No other devices were affected.

\subsubsection{Broadcast Deauthentication}

First, a broadcast deauth targeting all clients of the AP:

\begin{lstlisting}
sudo aireplay-ng -0 5 -a A6:8E:DC:82:EB:7B wlp2s0mon
\end{lstlisting}

This sends five deauth frames to the broadcast address, disconnecting all clients.

\subsubsection{Directed Deauthentication}

The first directed attempt failed because the monitor interface was on channel 13 instead of channel 6:

\begin{lstlisting}
sudo aireplay-ng -0 5 -a A6:8E:DC:82:EB:7B -c 14:5A:FC:81:7E:1D wlp2s0mon
\end{lstlisting}

Running \texttt{airmon-ng check kill} fixed the channel. The next attempt, against \texttt{24:EB:16:56:E1:CC}, worked:

\begin{lstlisting}
sudo aireplay-ng -0 5 -a A6:8E:DC:82:EB:7B -c 24:EB:16:56:E1:CC wlp2s0mon
\end{lstlisting}

\texttt{aireplay-ng} got 63 ACKs out of 67, confirming the client received the frames.

A continuous attack (\texttt{-0 0}) ran for five minutes. ACK counts varied as the client repeatedly tried to reconnect.

\subsubsection{Fake Authentication Attempt}

I also attempted a fake authentication:

\begin{lstlisting}
sudo aireplay-ng -1 0 -a A6:8E:DC:82:EB:7B -c 24:EB:16:56:E1:CC wlp2s0mon
\end{lstlisting}

The auth step succeeded, but association failed with status code 12 (\emph{wrong ESSID or WPA}). On an open network it would have succeeded, exposing traffic in plaintext.

Deauth frames in 802.11 are unauthenticated; anyone can send them. The 63/67 ACK rate confirms the client accepted them. WPA3 with PMF signs management frames and blocks this attack. Since almost no network in range used WPA3, this attack applies to nearly all of them.

\subsection{WPA Handshake Capture}

After the deauth, the client reconnected and the handshake appeared in the capture:

\begin{lstlisting}
WPA Handshake: A6:8E:DC:82:EB:7B
\end{lstlisting}

With the handshake, \texttt{aircrack-ng} or \texttt{hashcat} can test passphrases offline. A weak passphrase can be cracked in hours or days. Combined with the scan data, this is enough for unauthorised access, traffic monitoring, device tracking, and denial of service.

\subsection{Passphrase Cracking Attempt}

I ran \texttt{aircrack-ng} against the capture using the \texttt{rockyou.txt} wordlist:

\begin{lstlisting}
aircrack-ng -w /usr/share/wordlists/rockyou.txt capture-01.cap
\end{lstlisting}

It ran for 20 minutes, testing \textbf{14,344,391} candidates at ~\textbf{11,780 keys/second}. The passphrase \textbf{was not found}.

A passphrase not in common wordlists will not fall to a dictionary attack. Going further requires larger wordlists, GPU hardware, or significant compute time.

\section{Summary and Conclusion}

This assignment covered Wi-Fi reconnaissance with Aircrack-ng. Using \texttt{airmon-ng}, \texttt{airodump-ng}, and \texttt{aireplay-ng}, I scanned nearby networks and captured a WPA2 handshake.

Six sessions on 2026-05-16 (12:27--12:54) found 39 access points. All data was collected passively without joining any network. The deauth attack forced a reconnect and produced the handshake.

Notable findings: open hotspots, mixed WPA/WPA2 setups, minimal WPA3 adoption, identifiable IoT devices, and unauthenticated management frames.

\textbf{Takeaways:}

\begin{itemize}
    \item Passive scanning reveals SSIDs, BSSIDs, encryption, channels, signal, and vendor hints without any authentication.
    \item Unauthenticated management frames mean deauth attacks work against almost every WPA2 network. WPA3 with PMF is the only fix.
    \item Once captured, the handshake can be attacked offline indefinitely. A strong passphrase is the only defence.
    \item WPA3 adoption is slow. None of the 39 APs ran WPA3 exclusively.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Recommendations}

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Use WPA3 if possible, WPA2 at minimum.} WPA3's PMF blocks deauth attacks and SAE prevents offline cracking. Avoid mixed WPA/WPA2 modes, which fall back to TKIP.
    \item \textbf{Don't use open Wi-Fi.} Traffic is plaintext and visible to anyone in range.
    \item \textbf{Use a strong passphrase.} A passphrase not in common wordlists will not fall to a dictionary attack. Avoid real words and short phrases.
    \item \textbf{Keep firmware updated,} especially on ISP routers and IoT devices.
    \item \textbf{Disable WPS.} Its PIN is vulnerable to brute force regardless of the WPA2 passphrase.
    \item \textbf{Periodically check the client list} for unknown devices.
\end{itemize}

Wi-Fi reconnaissance surfaces weak spots before someone else finds them.
